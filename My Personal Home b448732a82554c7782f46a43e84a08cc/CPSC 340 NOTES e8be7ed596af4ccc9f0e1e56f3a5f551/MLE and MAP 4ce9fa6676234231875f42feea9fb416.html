Generative vs. Discriminative

  • discriminative model: p(y∣X,w)p(y|X,w)p(y∣X,w)﻿
    • max "+1-ness"
    • least squares, robust regression, logistic regression, etc.
    • can use complicated features as you don't model 'X': did MLE “conditioned” on the features ‘X’ being fixed (no “likelihood of X”)
  • generative model: p(y,X∣w)p(y,X|w)p(y,X∣w)﻿
    • max "data likelihood"
    • naive bayes, linear discriminant analysis (makes Gaussian assumption)
    • often need strong assumption as they model 'X'
    • generative models are often better with small ‘n’.

Overfitting

MLE often leads to overfitting:

  • Data could be very likely for some very unlikely 'w'
  • e.g., a complex model that overfits by memorizing the data

Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) Estimation

Maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate maximizes the reverse probability:

w^∈argmaxw{p(w∣D)}\hat{w}\in argmax_w\{p(w|D)\}w^∈argmaxw​{p(w∣D)}﻿

we want the probability of ‘w’ given our data

MLE and MAP are connected by Bayes rule:

MAP maximizes the likelihood p(D∣w)p(D|w)p(D∣w)﻿ times the prior p(w):

  1. Prior is our “belief” that ‘w’ is correct before seeing data.
  1. Prior can reflect that complex models are likely to overfit.
